PROJECT IMAGINE
Creating an immersive digital experience for High School Social Studies students and Teachers.
Process Overview

Social Studies Teachers need a Product that fits the C3 Framework. How do we engage students with 1st person sources of history while fitting the new state framework? As a UX designer, I researched and discovered 6 different digital templates for high school history teachers to engage students through perspective and empathy.

Empathize
Picking up market research

I started off by looking previous research that showed social studies teachers are creating their own coursework to fit state standards. The requirement is that we need to fit this new standard and capitilize on the first-to-market advantage

Focus groups

Now that we knew there was quantitatively a need for a new product that would fit state standards, we wanted to know more about the specific problems they were facing with products today. We held a few focus groups to get some more insight
What we did:
  • 8 Focus groups
  • 56 Social studies educators
  • 2 Prototypes presented
  • Remote, moderated, webcam
What we learned:
  • Teachers want to mix lecture and active learning
  • Students are comming in unprepared
  • Primary sources are cruical to History
  • Teachers vary in how the feel about content difficulty

Competitive Analysis

There are a ton of products that inspired us. To get a better idea of our scope, we looked at the learning potential and engagement factor of other products.
GOOGLE MAPS
What We Like
Provides Real World Context, Gives connection to current day, 3d environments feel immersive and exploratory, students can move to different areas
What we Didn't Like
Its too exploratory, can go off track to easily, Students may have to leap to get a good connection
NEW YORK TIMES PARALLAX
What We Like
The immersiveness of the story telling, the amazing content, the bone-chilling story, the visual effect of the parallax
What we Didn't Like
There is a lot of text, we feel as if students may not read it. The parallax can be distracting, it might be difficult to load all the images and videos
NATIONAL GEOGRAPHIC MAP MAKER
What We Like
Customizable for the teachers to author, Tips & Tricks section for better usage, Provides real context in terms of the map, Large Images, Use of primary source as a main learning point (the map itself), Twitter Connections
What we Didn't Like
Linear Path is not exploratory, Sometimes lost the proximity of a map that was too close, didn't like the red text.
PEARSON MY WORLD INTERACTIVE
What We Like
Students were engaged in creating their own product (User generated content), Provides ownership, Students who engage properly demonstrate mastery of the content.
What we Didn't Like
Students often go off track and miss the point, Teachers find it a hassle to grade all of them, Doesn’t feel immersive, Can be stressful for students who don’t have the skills or equipment, Doesn't match C3 Framework.
COMPANY OF HEROES 2
What We Like
Gamification, students love the video game elements, has realistic timeline integrated, is engaging, student feel empathy for character, minimalist UI.
What we Didn't Like
Slightly violent, afraid that student wont make connection, minimalist UI, teachers might be turned off, harder to sell.

Validate
User Testing

To validate our designs, we conducted a navigation based usability test, along with debrief interview questions.
What we did:
  • 5 Social Studies Teachers and 7 high school students
  • 4 Navigation based usability tasks
  • 4 Interview questions
  • In-person moderated study
What we learned:
  • Teachers loved the idea of using primary sources
  • Teachers were not a huge fan of the visual design (they wanted photographs and “high school” level illustrations)
  • Teachers wanted more different kinds of activities. They feel that writing is necessary for alot of cases, but sometimes they want to have more creative solutions.
  • Teachers feel as if the short answer questions are too open ended.
  • Students and teachers were unable to navigate back to an introduction video (hidden in a menu)
  • Students felt the material was at an appropriate level

Field Study

How will this product perform in the real world. Do social studies students and teachers use Project Imagine in a way that meets their needs? We conducted a field and daily journal study to see how users would use this product in the classroom. I lead the creation of the exit survey for students, and guided the general research goals.
What we did:
  • 3 Participating teachers (5 classes)
  • 90 Exit surveys
  • 359 completed daily journal entries
  • 5 Days of study
  • Asked list of general pros and cons for each day
What we learned:
  • Students felt the readings were too long
  • Students needed more guidance and instruction
  • Students liked being able to see how different people lived
  • Students enjoyed the videos, and wanted more
  • Students like the hands-on activities but thought they were too easy
  • Students had a hard time finding connections between what they did and the real-world

Implement
Key Insights

From the user test and the field study, we came up with some solutions to relieve the painpoints our users were facing. There were many minor usability issues, but below are some of the key problems and solutions.

Perspective Shift

From the usability study, we knew that perspective was key to feeling immersive. We switched the perspectives to make it feel like you are playing the character, rather than just watching them.
Animation & meaningful choices

There were some problems in the navigation, so we wanted to add some clairty in progression. We added animations to show movement from point A to point B. We also added "wrong choices" for some responses to add both guidance and challenge for students. I also added a progress indicator to provide orientation, to help students with timing.
Variation in activities

Many teachers wanted variation in activities, so we created more.
Photography visual direction

Finally, our users were complaining about the lack of sophistication in the illustrations and images. We changed from illustrations to photography, and in the field test, people generally liked the visuals.

Next steps & reflection
Iterative product cycles rarely ever end. Post release of the product, we will capture quantitative analysis. Most of the preliminary research was qualitative in nature, and while it answers the “why” factor, it does not answer the “how.”

The product process could have been improved in a few ways. We jumped right into designing all 6 different templates for project imagine. Some of the issues from one template were projected onto all of the templates, but they went undiscovered until testing. It is important to find large scale issues in small components quickly so that we don’t have to go back to change all of them.

Additionally, there were many limitations of the platform that are currently being updated to better support different products. Since the teams work in parallel, we needed to release the product before some platform features could support what we needed. In future iterations we would like to take advantage of these updates to the platform to better fit our needs.
