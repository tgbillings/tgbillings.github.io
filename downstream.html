Downstream

Downstream is a 2D game where you play as Current, a koi fish trying to find her lover, Tide. As the evening grows, the waters become increasingly dangerous. Current must Illuminate any lotus flowers she passes to find her way to Tide.
Game design goals
Downstream is an action mini-game which has strong timing and precision components but also a fun level-based story progression. As a minimalist 2 button experience, the game should be attractive to casual gamers while also enticing some more serious gamers with its high-level timing challenges. Beautifully drawn and transitioning artwork will provide players a sense of immersion. The fast paced gameplay means the player is constantly moving and reacting to new challenges. Timed mechanics keeps players on their toes. In order to make player choice meaningful, we provide multiple paths to victory. Rather than a linear map for each level, level compel the player to explore and move in different directions. Some paths will be easier or quicker than others, but there are different strategies for approaching challenges as they arise.
Gameplay specifications
Gameplay actions

There are 3 gameplay actions. Launch, which allows the player to launch themselves off a lily pad in a tangent to their current location on the lily pad rotation. Latch, which allows a player to attach themselves to a lily pad and start swimming in a circle around it Burst, which increases the fishes speed to 3 times the current speed, but is limited by the burst gauge.
Gameplay interactions

There are a number of gameplay interactions. Interactions are gameplay elements that the player does not directly control. They define the interaction that the player experiences that they do not directly impact with their controls or buttons.
Game challenges
Tethers

Koi uses tethers to get around a level. These can be lily pads or lotus flowers. To move about, Koi catches a tether to start circling it and releases to launch tangentially off of it. Players have to predict the tangent that they will take to successfully move through the level.
Enemies

Predators lurk under the water, waiting to eat anything which crosses their paths. Koi must avoid these enemies by charting her path carefully. Some fish will patrol in a pattern while others may be unpredictable. When an enemy hits Koi, she loses a life. The light of a lit lotus flower will scare away the fish and keep Koi safe.
Obstacles

Whirlpools and rocks await the unwary Koi as she swims towards the sea. Collision objects blocking Koiâ€™s path will kill her if she hits them, while moving obstacles like whirlpools change her path. These could be predictable or unpredictable path changes. Diverting obstacles give the game a stronger spatial reasoning component.
Lotus flowers

Koi has to light lotus flowers before passing through the end gate. She does this by circling them to help them bloom and expose the fire they contain. The player will have to be quick when encountering these more volatile lotuses. Circling a lotus for too long can be dangerous due to obstacles, so Koi will have to leave and return to reignite it. Lotus flowers are essential to the progression of the game, providing its destination-based story a puzzle-based gameplay mechanic as the player progresses.
Level design patterns
There are 3 main types of patterns that the players must overcome. In early levels, the player learns the mechanics and faces some of these patterns independently. In later levels, we use composite patterns and combine multiple types of challenges into one.

Threading the Needle

Threading the Needle is getting through a tight space. Players can take as much time as they want but must be precise in their aiming. This takes precision and prediction skills. You must be able to predict where Koi will go, and aim precisely.
Hot potato

The player must jump from lily to lily quickly. Does not have to be precise but needs to react quickly. This takes reaction time and speed, along with minimal precision. You must be able to get off the current lily quickly before you lose. In the example below, if the player stays on the lily too long, they will crash into the rock.
Danger zones

Players must be able to predict the movement enemy fish, and adapt to it appropriately. They also need to use Lotuses effectively to create safe spaces and clear paths that may be too difficult to clear when enemy fish are swimming.
Leap of faith

We reward players for taking a chance and explore different options. Usually this is in the form of a faster way to the finish line. This means that curious players will do much better on average.
Composite patterns

In harder levels, we introduce the players to composite patterns for more of a challenge. We combine any number of these challenges together. Research & Releases
Sketch testing

At first, there were two different concepts we wanted to explore. The first was a Space concept, where players would control a spaceship and abduct cows by orbiting the planet, and the second was a Koi fish concept where the player swims in a pond and lights up lily pads by swimming around them. We tested both concepts, through simple comparison. We asked the players which they preferred. 12 of the 32 players preferred the river concept, and 20 preferred the cow space concept. Even though the cow space concept had a higher quantity, the artists decided the river concept was more fun, and provided greater creative flexibility.
Non-digital prototype

We first tested a non-digital version of the designs to see if they were valid or not. We created a cardboard prototype to test with around 30 players. The paper prototype was used to capture different game elements to test at a low cost. We found that players enjoyed the prediction aspect of the game, where users are trying to predict the outcome before they move spaces forward. Players did not like the slow pace of the paper prototype.
Alpha release

In Alpha testing, we tested the core game mechanics of Tethering and Latching. In the original design, we had players hold the spacebar to attach, and let go of the spacebar to detach. We found this control system worked very poorly.
Beta release

For Beta release, we had the bulk of code finished. Some key changes were made were changing the control scheme. Rather than holding space, the user simply taps space to attach, and taps space to detach. We also added other features to the game, such as whirlpools, enemy fish, and more level design.
Final release

For our final release we focused on level design. We needed to make sure that the levels were challenging, and fun, while still hitting the design goals of the game. The project accumulated in a game design showcase allowing the public to play our game.
