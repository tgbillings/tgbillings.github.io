What is Kids colab?

Kids colab is a co-operative approach to design that involves one of our target users (students) in the design process. It is a way of participatory design that ensures that our students are heard. The kids co-lab group meets weekly during the school year for 2 hours per week, and have a 1 week summer camp, which they meet for 6 hours a day, every day during the week. The co-lab group consists of 12 middle school aged children who provide feedback for pearson products. I was a key player in making this effort successful by moderating sessions and helping students execute their designs. I also provided analysis and insight after each session to report to the product teams that were testing or gaining information about their designs.

Why is participatory design important?

Participatory design is a process, and not a style. In point of participatory design is not to have users design products, but to gain insight as to why they might want to make a certain design choices. It allows for a better cultural and societal understanding of their day-to-day problems and the mental models that lead to certain solutions. It does not help pearson to know that students want to design a magical home-work creating robot, but the invaluable information is the stress and pressure that students feel from their parents, teachers, and peers when it comes to completing their homework on time while maintaining a social life balance, all while trying to look “cool.”

What happens in a colab session?

There are many different ways we utilize the time spent with the students. Almost all of these research sessions are Qualitative, direct and attitudinal. The sessions differ depending on what research question needs to be answered. One of the most common sessions is showing the students a current pearson design, and asking them to make changes, and document why each change is made. Another example is having students design their own solution to a problem, such as designing a new way to read your textbook, or design a new way to learn math that isn’t through a video, teacher or textbook.

Methodology

The Kids Colab team (11 middle grade students, myself & director of research) were asked to design a student website that might be a part of a Pearson product. First, the students brainstormed what they’d like to see on a student webpage. Then, we asked students to create a website including those features using a Layered Elaboration approach.

Layered Elaboration is a technique using transparencies.

  • Each group creates the homepage using the transparency adding the must have and desired elements.
  • All groups come back together for a “stand up meeting” where each group briefly presents their ideas to the other group.
  • The kid design partners rotate to the next group while the adult partners stay on their page. Put a clear transparency on top of group 1’s revisions, along with the “2” in the upper right hand corner and names/initials of the group members.
  • New groups elaborate on the initial idea by adding to, editing, and changing the first groups ideas. However, all deletions are done by crossing out ideas instead of erasing them, thus leaving all iterations in tact.
  • Continue with stand-up meetings and new transparencies iteratively until each group has had a chance to elaborate on each paper.
  • Photos are being taken after each layer.

Key take aways

The following features were present in all designs:
  1. Access to all subjects.
  2. Links to resources (videos, notebook, grades, 3rd-party websites)
  3. Assignments/due dates, “I need to do xyz and I need to see when due.”
  4. Help button
All kids designed page to be simple with alot of white space. While the placement of resources and subjects differed between groups, all agreed that easy access to ALL subjects and ALL resources on home page and not a link to a seperate screen/page. Students expect the website to be a place they can go to, to access their assignments, and they also expect assistance in completing their assignments in close proximity, such as a link with how to solve their problems, or a video that is specific to the assignment.

Most kids wanted the following features:
  • Areas divided/broken out by subject area
  • Quick access to Class/Homework Assignments
  • Links to Khan Academy
  • Links to Games
  • Links to Google Docs/Classrooms
  • Videos (Support/Review/Summaries)
  • Group Chat/Communication with Teacher
  • Assignment due dates/Reminders
  • View assignments by subject area
  • View resources by subject area
  • Access to Quizzes/Tests
Some kids wanted the following features:
  • Ability to customize (i.e., use a nickname)
  • Games
  • Humor element
  • Greeting (i.e., Welcome)
  • Entire textbook
Elementary school games
The Elementary Language Arts Team would like to gauge the age level appropriateness of the game, Urchin Search.

First, the Kids Colab team evaluated the game in small groups. Each kid got several minutes to play the game. They were asked their likes, dislikes, and areas of confusion. The kids were instructed to think about the gameplay and format of the game, not the content.

Then, students were asked to rate the general appeal of the game on a 4-point scale. They were also asked to choose all the grade levels for which they felt the game was appropriate.

Next, the Colab team was asked to make specific modifications to the game to make it appropriate for grades 3rd - 5th. They were asked which elements must be change, would be nice to change, and don’t need to change to make appropriate for grades 3-5.

Research Questions
1
What is the suitability/age appropriateness of the game (characters, voice, sound, background, and theme)

2
How can game be modified to make appropriate for grades 3rd-5th?

Game appeal

Grade level appropriateness

Key factors in grade level appropriateness

The game was appropraite for a kindergarten and 1st grade because it was simple to follow, easy to follow along and there was nothing to confuse you. There are not alot of distractions, and all the levels are exactly the same format with little variation. The characters are also geared towards younger kinds, and did not feel right for 3rd to 5th graders. To make it more grade appropriate, the characters could be more complex, and the levels could vary more than just finding words. The students were able to pick up on the idea that this is just a multiple choice quiz with a different visual.

Key take away

    Overall:
  • Kids want more human-like/realistic characters
  • Kids want an end-goal/purpose for the game/backstory - “Save the Sea Urchins”
  • More obvious score/point system with rewards
  • Simple/straightforward games can work/have merit
  • Include instructions/directions
    Voices:
  • Noises need to be made more mature/more age-appropriate
  • Less Robotic
  • Different voices at every level/More variety
  • Less annoying
    Sounds:
  • More realistic/coordinate with theme
  • Specific sounds for different characters
  • Bubble sounds can stay/appealing
    Background:
  • More consistency
  • More interesting/variety
  • Didn’t get stem/leaves
    Theme:
  • More complex theme eg “Star Search” (Outerspace), Shipwreck - finding treasure
  • Don’t mix themes
  • Connect with topics in class
